RQ 1 Chatbot Style

Which elements of the chatbot's communication style (e.g. asking questions, suggesting solutions, showing understanding) do users experience as helpful – and which as insufficient? qualitative

Short Answer

Justly's current chatbot style is experienced as insufficient by 5 out of 5 participants. Empathy and understanding alone do not create perceived value. No aspect of the current style was experienced as genuinely helpful – participants could only describe what they would wish for instead.

What Was Experienced as Insufficient
negative
Too much reflexive empathy, too little substance 5 / 5
The AI repeats, validates, and shows compassion – without offering concrete prompts for reflection or practical guidance. The tone feels forced and inauthentic: "All soft and flattering – no real person talks like that" (Renate). Too many affirming responses without real content make the AI immediately recognisable as AI (Mia). All 5 participants found this inauthentic or without value.
negative
No change of perspective
The AI mirrors back what was said instead of introducing new viewpoints. Sophie: "Fake conversation – nothing but empathy, no substance."
negative
No memory — and a tendency to amplify negative patterns
Lukas experienced the AI forgetting things said moments earlier while simultaneously constructing negative patterns that did not reflect his actual experience — in his case, an unwarranted focus on problems in a relationship he viewed positively. Rather than helping, this risked worsening his perspective.
What Users Would Want Instead
wish
Matter-of-fact, neutral counterpart
Sophie would see the AI as a "factual information source" – someone who does not simulate a relationship but instead provides orientation and context.
wish
Concrete questions and prompts for reflection
Several participants wished the AI would ask more follow-up questions, draw connections, and broaden their perspective.
wish
Context-dependent empathy
Mia: The empathetic tone is appropriate on a hard day – but overdone for everyday topics. A more differentiated style would be more convincing.
wish
Personas concept
Claudia and Mia welcomed the idea of choosing between different AI personas — but primarily because they were dissatisfied with the current style and hoped a different persona would interact with them differently.
"Ideally, I'd see an AI like this more as a factual information source, not as a relationship object. Someone who simply has no experience with this – it carries no weight."
Sophie, f, 26 yrs
"It tries to say things very empathetically, even though it's not appropriate. You'd have to differentiate – when you're having a hard time, versus when it's actually just a normal day."
Mia, f, 24 yrs
"The AI is all soft and flattering. No real person talks like that."
Renate, f, 62 yrs
Conclusion

The chatbot needs a fundamental redesign of its conversational approach. It should ask concrete questions, introduce new perspectives, and avoid simply mirroring what users say. Critically, it must not construct or amplify negative patterns that don't reflect the user's actual experience — as Lukas's case shows, this can worsen rather than help. The style should adapt to context (a difficult moment calls for a different tone than an ordinary day), and ideally allow users to choose their preferred conversational mode. Empathy alone is not enough — substance, memory, and accuracy are what build genuine perceived value.

RQ 2 Post-Chat Summary

Which elements of the automatically generated post-chat summary (e.g. emotions, challenges, next steps) do users find meaningful and helpful – and which do they not? qualitative

Short Answer

The automatic emotion rating (pie chart) is rejected by 5 out of 5 participants. Challenges presented as bullet points are rated positively. The summary as a whole generates little motivation to revisit it.

What Was Experienced as Not Helpful
negative
Automatic emotion rating (pie chart) 5 / 5
All participants rejected the automatically assigned overall mood. It felt arbitrary, opaque, and inaccurate.
  • Lukas: generic value assignments that did not reflect his experience
  • Renate: "Enthusiasm" rated too low; visualisation not intuitive
  • Sophie: overdramatic, cannot be adjusted – "not credible after a short conversation"
  • Claudia: would give herself 90% enthusiasm
  • Mia: a 70% mood score after 10 messages feels arbitrary
negative
Summary in someone else's words
Sophie would not re-read the summary – "because those aren't my words."
negative
No motivation to revisit 4 / 5
4 out of 5 participants would not reopen the summary.
What Was Actually Experienced as Helpful
positive
Challenges as bullet points
Sophie and Mia praised the clear at-a-glance format. Reframing problems as "challenges" was seen as potentially helpful.
positive
Accurate content summary
Renate and Claudia felt the AI had correctly captured the essence of their situation – this was perceived positively.
positive
Long-term calendar / mood trend
Mia appreciated the monthly calendar with colour-coded days in the profile – "That's great." Tracking mood across multiple sessions would be more credible than a single-session score.
"I find that a bit overdramatic – especially because the app assigned it to me. And maybe I don't even feel that way."
Sophie, f, 26 yrs
"I have no idea where it gets that my overall mood is 70 – I find that strange after just 10 messages."
Mia, f, 24 yrs
Conclusion

The automatic emotion rating should be replaced by a user-controlled input: let users choose and name their own emotions. This gives them agency over how they are represented — and at the same time supports emotional competence, helping users become more aware of and better able to identify their own emotional states. Challenges as bullet points and an accurate content summary are strengths that should be retained, as long as the actual chat remains accessible.

RQ 3 Features & Navigation

Which features outside the chat (e.g. themed journals, pre-written prompts, weekly insights, goals) would users actually use – and which feel overwhelming or unnecessary? qualitative

Short Answer

The app offers genuinely valuable features – routines, journal variety, the open journal, and the personas concept are well received. However, access to these features is significantly hindered by unclear navigation (5 / 5) and early paywalls (5 / 5).

Features That Would Actually Be Used
Feature Mentioned by Why Helpful
Open Journal (without AI) Lukas, Renate The freedom to choose between AI chat and free writing is explicitly valued; Renate would prefer the open journal over the AI chat
Routines (yoga, meditation, habits) Renate, Mia Renate was excited by the options; Mia: "Routines are enormously important" – a clear use case
Themed Journals (variety) Renate, Mia Many topics resonate: self-confidence, decision clarity, travel, worries – broad relevance
Personas Concept (choose AI style) Mia, Claudia The idea of choosing the AI's conversational style is understood and seen as meaningful
Calendar with marked days (profile) Mia, Sophie Monthly overview of emotional mood; visual trend over time is more credible than a single value; Sophie would prefer a neutral marking option to avoid seeing too many sad-face days
Challenges as Bullet Points Sophie, Mia Clear at-a-glance format; reframing as a "challenge" can be motivating
Features That Feel Overwhelming or Unconvincing
negative
Explore section entirely subscription-only
Lukas discovered that the entire Explore section is behind a paywall. This makes exploring the app frustrating before the core value can even be experienced.
negative
Hidden paywall in the journal list
Sophie: The journal list appears free – only the final tap reveals the payment screen. The feeling of being misled reduces willingness to engage with the app.
negative
Icons without labels in navigation 5 / 5
All participants struggled with unclear icons. Features were overlooked or misidentified because context was missing (e.g. star icon = Insights, but mistaken for AI chat by Mia; dream journal icon unclear to Claudia).
negative
"Continue" button in the chat
Sophie: The button should say "Send" – the current label makes it unclear what it does.
negative
Calendar navigated to wrong day
Lukas spent five minutes confused because it was not clear that he had navigated to a future day where no session could be started (no visual indicator).
negative
No visible value after the first session 4 / 5
4 out of 5 participants saw no reason to reopen the app after completing their session. The home screen appeared empty after entries were made.
"It's just one long list. The payment page only opens when you tap 'Use'. It wasn't clear at all beforehand that these were all premium items."
Sophie, f, 26 yrs
"I had to tap on it first to understand what it was. With the journals, it really wasn't clear when I tapped it – I just thought there was nothing there."
Renate, f, 62 yrs
"What do I actually get from having entered my goals for today? On the home screen I still see nothing."
Lukas, m, 33 yrs
Conclusion

The well-received features – routines, journal variety, open journal, personas – could be positioned as stronger entry points. Navigation needs short text labels below the icons. Premium content must be clearly marked as such upfront, and the free experience must already deliver genuine value. Session-start buttons should be greyed out when the calendar is showing a past or future day. Rename the "Continue" button in the chat to "Send" to better communicate what it does.

RQ 4 Willingness to Pay

Would users regularly use the app or pay for it? quantitative  And why / why not? qualitative

Short Answer

4 out of 5 participants would neither use Justly regularly nor pay for it. Only Mia showed a basic willingness to pay and is the only participant who expressed interest in using the app regularly.

Quantitative Overview
Participant Use regularly (1–5) NPS (0–10) Willingness to pay
Lukas 1 0 No – "They'd have to pay me."
Renate 1 1 No – "I would never do that. Never."
Sophie 1 0–1 No
Claudia 1 2 No
Mia 4 8 Possibly – for a limited period; €8/week too high for long-term use
Why Is Willingness to Pay Absent?
negative
No perceived value
Those who have not experienced the app's benefit – because the free experience is too limited or the first session lacks real depth – find no price justifiable.
negative
Paywall too early and too hidden
Users became frustrated before they could even discover the app's core value. This increases distrust toward the pricing model.
negative
AI not accepted as a substitute for real support 5 / 5
All 5 participants do not see an AI app as a replacement for human connection or therapy – which reduces the perceived need to pay for it. Claudia and Sophie expressed active concerns that the app could make people lonelier.
negative
No motivation to return after the first session
If the app creates no incentive to come back after one session, there is no felt need for a subscription.
Why Did Mia Show Willingness to Pay?
positive
Reframing as genuine perceived value
The post-chat summary reframed Mia's everyday stress as a "challenge she was mastering" rather than a burden. She experienced this shift in perspective as genuine value – and it was enough to make her consider paying for the app, at least short-term.
"I would never do that. Never."
Renate, f, 62 yrs
"They'd have to pay me."
Lukas, m, 33 yrs
"It can be a great support tool for someone who's already in therapy, as an add-on. You just have to see it as a tool."
Mia, f, 24 yrs
Conclusion

The app needs to create value before it asks for it. A more compelling freemium experience (longer trial period, deeper first session without a message limit) would likely improve conversion. Mia demonstrates that even a well-designed reframing in the summary – stress as a "challenge" rather than a problem – can create genuine perceived value. This is a scalable lever: the benefit does not have to depend on an emotionally exceptional moment; it can emerge in everyday life when the app shifts the user's perspective on their own situation.
